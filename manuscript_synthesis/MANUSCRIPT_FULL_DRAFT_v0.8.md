# The metabolic geometry of severe human neutrophils: redox–carbon state separation and the limits of inferring effector function

*A stock-like observable cannot identify the flows that generate it; each molecular layer measures a
partial truth about the neutrophil, and the error would be to force them all to say the same thing.*

---

## Abstract

**Severe inflammatory disease reorganizes the metabolic state of the human neutrophil.** In a human
neutrophil metabolome across COVID-19 severity (Li_ST002477; 287 metabolites × 75 samples), the severity
groups occupy distinguishable metabolic states by whole-distribution tests (Control/Mild/Severe;
permutation p≈0.001 for all three comparisons), severity-ordered with the largest separation between
Control and Severe. Severity is thus expressed as a shift of the whole metabolic state — a metabolic
geometry — rather than as a single altered marker, and the stable contributors concentrate in a
glycolysis/PPP/central-carbon, redox-linked module (M05). These are state-associated metabolites, not evidence of metabolic flux.

A second layer describes what the activated neutrophil releases. In a human RA/SLE NET/release proteome
(Chapman_PXD011796), the released material is a structured, chemically edited effector architecture — a
chromatin/histone scaffold, granule proteases, an MPO/oxidative module, cytoskeletal proteins and nuclear
autoantigens, with citrullination, acetylation and methylation reaching beyond histones to granule and
cytoskeletal proteins.
Read only at the level of modules, the carbon-redox metabolic axis and this oxidative–chromatin–granule material converge as a hypothesis-generating context alignment, not a co-measured coupling, metabolic flux, NETosis, enzyme activity or causal link.

What these state and composition layers cannot do is identify the latent flows. NET burden — cfDNA,
MPO-DNA, citrullinated histone H3 — is itself a stock-like accumulated observable, and with a deliberately
favourable observation model we show that a single cross-sectional burden measurement identifies only the
ratio of formation to removal, not formation and removal separately ("stocks are not flows").
A measured state likewise does not by itself identify flux or executed function.
That removal is real and variable, not hypothetical, is anchored in reports of impaired serum NET
degradation in systemic lupus erythematosus subsets (associated with lupus nephritis) and of
NET-degradation capacity co-varying with disease activity within patients over up to five years.

We therefore derive a table of measurement breakers: for each ambiguity, the minimal co-measurement that
separates the latent mechanisms (e.g., paired NET-formation and serum-degradation assays). The breakers
are design requirements, not demonstrated mechanisms, and they frame a testable hypothesis: paired
measurement of NET formation and serum NET-degradation/clearance capacity will determine whether
high-burden inflammatory profiles are formation-dominant, clearance-limited, mixed or neither —
distinctions a single cross-sectional NET-burden measurement cannot make.
We do not claim that NET burden is uninformative, that NETosis cannot be measured, that clearance dominates, that formation is unimportant, or that any disease mechanism, clinical utility, metabolic flux, executed function or validated biomarker is established here.

## Introduction

Neutrophils are central effectors of inflammation. In sepsis, severe COVID-19, and systemic
autoimmunity they migrate, degranulate, remodel their metabolism, and release neutrophil
extracellular traps (NETs) — webs of chromatin, histones and granule enzymes that can both contain
pathogens and injure host tissue (Brinkmann et al. 2004, DOI: 10.1126/science.1092385;
Papayannopoulos 2018, DOI: 10.1038/nri.2017.105). Yet the neutrophil is often collapsed into a single
status —
"activated," "primed," or one marker of degranulation or NET formation. This loses what makes the
cell interesting: its biology is distributed across molecular layers that carry different information
on different timescales. Phosphosignaling acts within seconds to minutes, before new genes are
transcribed; metabolic state integrates over minutes to hours; the released NET/release material is
what the cell deposits outside itself; and the transcriptional/multiome layer organizes its cell
states. A measurement on one layer is informative about that layer alone, and a label from one does
not transfer to the others — which is why a single molecular layer is insufficient to describe the
neutrophil in inflammatory disease, a cell now recognised as transcriptionally and functionally
heterogeneous (Ng, Ostuni and Hidalgo 2019; DOI: 10.1038/s41577-019-0141-8).

Public repositories now hold neutrophil datasets on each layer, but they come from different studies,
species, diseases and donors, and were not measured in the same cells. They also do not share one
severity scale: quantitative severity here is anchored in a human neutrophil metabolome (a COVID-19
series), while the other layers report human NET/release material in autoimmune disease, a primarily
mouse-derived cell-state architecture, and human stimulus-specific signaling. Stitching them together
— reading a transcriptional state, a metabolic state and a NET phenotype as if they belonged to one
cell — is not warranted: with unpaired datasets, many cross-layer relationships are equally compatible
with the same separate measurements, so a forced combination invents a relationship the data cannot
establish.

We take the opposite approach: treat each layer as an independent window onto a different aspect of
the neutrophil, name its cell/species/disease/sample type explicitly, and keep explicit what the data
shows and what it cannot conclude (a metabolite level is a state, not a flux; a protein inventory is
composition, not a rate; a transcriptional program is potential, not executed function). Cross-layer
connections are biological hypotheses for paired experiments, not combined measurements. We anchor the
quantitative description on the strongest layer — a real human neutrophil metabolome across severity —
and place it alongside the human NET/release proteome and its modifications, the conserved cell-state
architecture, and the rapid phosphosignaling layer. Figure 1 provides a navigation map for the
manuscript, distinguishing the measured metabolic-state evidence from the compositional, reference and
article-level layers, and making explicit that the synthesis does not rely on sample-level fusion.

The layers are intentionally heterogeneous in disease context, species and evidence type; they are not
fused as if co-measured, but used to ask what each public evidence layer can and cannot say about
general neutrophil biology — not a single disease-specific model and not a proof of cross-layer
coupling. The metabolic dataset and its primary biological context come from Li et al. (2023); the
present work does not claim to rediscover that biology. Its distinct contribution is to formalise a
distributional state-separation geometry (a whole-distribution question, rather than the marker-level
findings reported there), to place that measured result within an explicit evidence-level hierarchy,
and to use it as the measured anchor for a broader, claim-limited multi-layer reading of
neutrophil-associated inflammatory states.

The manuscript follows this order, biology first: we show how severe inflammatory disease reorganizes the neutrophil metabolic state, read the released material as a structured effector architecture, and only then make explicit the identifiability limit on inferring effector function. The framing also leads to a directly testable hypothesis: if NET burden is a stock-like observable, then
paired measurement of NET formation and serum NET-degradation/clearance capacity should determine whether
high-burden profiles are formation-dominant, clearance-limited, mixed or neither — distinctions collapsed by
burden-only readouts. The purpose of this manuscript is therefore not to claim a validated biomarker or a
clinical mechanism, but to define the observation model, the evidence ceilings, and the measurement
breakers (Table 2) required to test that hypothesis.

## Methods / approach to evidence integration

**Levels of evidence (defined here, so the reading is self-contained).** Because we draw on
independent public datasets of different kinds, we first state plainly what *kind* of evidence each
layer is, since each kind licenses different statements. We distinguish four levels, ordered by how
directly the evidence is measured in this study:

1. **Measured quantitative result** — a real data matrix that we re-analyse here. It can support a
   measured state and its separation between groups. *In this work: the human neutrophil metabolome
   (Li_ST002477) is the only measured result.*
2. **Structured compositional evidence** — curated identity/composition tables that we parse but do
   not re-measure. They can support *what is present* (an inventory and its annotations), not how fast
   or how active a process is. *In this work: the NET/release proteome and its modifications
   (Chapman_PXD011796).*
3. **Reference architecture** — an external atlas used as an interpretive frame, not a measurement in
   our samples. It can support *how states are organised*, not co-measurement in our cohorts and not
   function. *In this work: a primarily mouse-derived neutrophil cell-state atlas with reported human
   conservation (NeuMap).*
4. **Published article-level evidence** — a result reported in a primary paper without a reusable data
   table. It can support *biological context*, not a new measurement here. *In this work: rapid
   phosphosignaling (PXD029046).*

The strength of a statement therefore follows the level it rests on (1 > 2 > 3 > 4), and **every level
is additionally bound by an explicit claim ceiling** — the strongest claim the data type itself
permits (a metabolite level is a state, not a flux; a protein inventory is composition, not a rate; a
modification is presence, not enzyme activity; a transcriptional program is potential, not executed
function; a signaling state is not downstream function). No level licenses cross-layer causal coupling
or sample-level fusion. These per-source levels and ceilings are summarised in Table 1 and, for every
source, in the supplementary evidence-level and claim-ceiling tables.

**Sources, named by cell/species/disease/sample type.** We assembled four primary neutrophil-related
molecular layers, and retained two contextual sources (GSE141285 mouse tissue-resident chromatin;
Sadiku_PXD061065 single-cell protein inventory) for boundary mapping (Table 1; Supplementary
evidence-level and claim-ceiling tables):
- **Li_ST002477** — human neutrophil metabolomics in COVID-19 (Metabolomics Workbench study ST002477;
  neutrophils isolated from severe/mild patients and healthy controls)
  (Li et al., Nat Commun 2023; DOI: 10.1038/s41467-023-37567-w; PMID 37147288; ST002477).
- **Chapman_PXD011796** — human NET/release proteome and modification tables in RA/SLE
  (Chapman et al., Front Immunol 2019;10:423; DOI: 10.3389/fimmu.2019.00423; PMID 30915077; PXD011796).
- **NeuMap** — a primarily mouse-derived neutrophil-compartment cell-state atlas with reported human
  conservation; used as a reference architecture, not co-measured with Li or Chapman
  (Cerezo-Wallis et al., Nature 2026;649(8098):1003-1012; DOI: 10.1038/s41586-025-09807-0).
- **PXD029046** — human stimulus-specific phosphosignaling, at the published-article level
  (Yedehalli Thimmappa et al., Cell Tissue Res 2022;389:241-257; DOI: 10.1007/s00441-022-03636-7; PXD029046).

We did not discard sources lacking a quantitative matrix, and we did not over-state any layer.

**Metabolic layer (the quantitative severity anchor).** We retrieved the Li_ST002477 human neutrophil
metabolite matrix (287 metabolites × 75 samples; 19 Control, 30 Mild, 26 Severe) and applied QC (zero
missing and zero non-numeric values; Blank/QC excluded). To ask whether severity groups occupy the
same or different metabolic spaces, we used two complementary whole-distribution tests — maximum mean
discrepancy (Gaussian kernel; bandwidth scales 0.5, 1, 2, 4) (Gretton et al. 2012; JMLR 13:723-773)
and energy distance (Székely and Rizzo 2013; DOI: 10.1016/j.jspi.2013.03.018) — each with
1000-permutation significance and leave-one-out stability. We described contributors with rank-based
statistics (Kruskal–Wallis with Benjamini–Hochberg FDR control
(Benjamini and Hochberg 1995; DOI: 10.1111/j.2517-6161.1995.tb02031.x); Cliff's delta effect size
(Cliff 1993; DOI: 10.1037/0033-2909.114.3.494)) and 500-resample bootstrap, and mapped them to
metabolic modules.

**Released-material layer.** We parsed the open-access Chapman supplementary tables (human RA/SLE)
into a structured NET/release proteome (gene-symbol module mapping) and a modified-material layer
(citrullination, acetylation, methylation). Modification entries are presence on the material, not
enzyme activity.

**Cell-state layer.** We confirmed the deposited NeuMap datasets and used the open marker table to
recover the marker genes organizing neutrophil cell states into programs (hubs). The hub markers use
mouse gene symbols (e.g., Ltf, Mmp8, mKi67, H2-Ab1); we use the atlas as a reference for conserved
state architecture, not as a human, sample-matched layer.

**Signaling layer.** We documented the PXD029046 human phosphosignaling evidence at the
published-article level; a reusable phosphosite table was not openly available.

**Integration principle.** We read the layers as complementary windows, each on a different timescale
and from a distinct cell/species/disease context, connected only conceptually and at the module level.
We did not combine datasets as if measured in the same cells, and we did not assert that any layer
causes another.

## Results

### A multi-layer neutrophil resource, honestly named
We assembled four primary neutrophil-related layers — metabolism (human, COVID-19), released material
(human, RA/SLE), cell-state architecture (primarily mouse-derived, human-conserved) and signaling
(human) — plus two contextual sources, and recorded for each its cell/species/disease and what its
data can and cannot support (Table 1; Figure 1). Throughout, each layer is read through the observation-model lens of Figure 2 (developed below): as a measured state or composition, not as a latent flow.

### A separable metabolic state in severity (the quantitative empirical anchor)
Li_ST002477 is the quantitative empirical anchor of the manuscript: it shows how severe inflammatory disease is expressed as a whole-distribution neutrophil metabolic state separation, while also illustrating that a measured state does not by itself identify flux, executed function or clinical mechanism. It is the only layer with a
quantitative severity axis. The severity groups occupied distinguishable metabolic states: both
whole-distribution tests were permutation-significant (p≈0.001) for all three comparisons, agreed 3/3,
were stable to removing any single sample, and were severity-ordered — the largest separation was
between Control and Severe (Figure 3). Among contributors, 183 of 287 metabolites differed across
groups (FDR < 0.05), 116 changed monotonically with severity, and 39 were resampling-stable; they
concentrated in a glycolysis/PPP/central-carbon module (M05), while lipid mediators (M08) were not
represented in this panel (Figure 4). These are state-associated metabolites, not evidence of flux. This
places the severity-associated metabolic state near an experimentally established neutrophil redox axis: in
activated neutrophils, a rapid switch toward a cyclic pentose phosphate mode can power NADPH oxidase-driven
oxidative burst (Britt et al. 2022, Nat Metab 4:389-403; DOI: 10.1038/s42255-022-00550-8; PMID 35347316).
Because our metabolomic layer does not co-measure isotope flux, NADPH turnover, ROS, NETosis or pathogen
killing in the same samples, M05 is read as a state-associated metabolic module, not as evidence of flux or
executed function; Britt supports biological plausibility of the glycolysis/PPP/redox axis, not flux in our
data.

### What the neutrophil releases: a structured, chemically edited effector architecture
The human RA/SLE NET/release tables describe not a random protein dump but a structured effector
architecture. Of 752 released proteins (272 healthy control, 480 RA/SLE), 141 mapped to curated
effector-material categories assigned by neutrophil compartment (Figure 5; a structured, curated module assignment, not a statistical enrichment test): a chromatin/histone scaffold,
granule proteases, an MPO/oxidative module, cytoskeletal proteins, and nuclear proteins that are also
recognized autoantigens in autoimmune disease. The released material is moreover chemically edited: the
modification tables added 87 citrullinated peptide entries (46 proteins), 60 acetylation entries
(49 proteins) and 16 methylation entries (12 proteins). This editing is not confined to chromatin —
histone H3 is represented across all three modification classes (a richly modified chromatin component, of
a kind associated with NET chromatin biology; Wang et al. 2009; DOI: 10.1083/jcb.200806072) — but the
representative modified proteins also include granule proteases (e.g. cathepsin G, elastase, azurocidin),
cytoskeletal proteins (actins, spectrin) and the oxidative enzyme myeloperoxidase (methylated). The
released material is therefore a structured, multi-compartment, post-translationally edited effector
inventory rather than chromatin alone.
This describes the composition and chemical editing of the released material and is bounded accordingly: it does not establish co-occurrence of the three modifications on the same peptide/residue, a NETosis or modification rate, enzyme (PAD4/HAT/HDAC/PRMT) activity, clearance, pathogenicity, immunogenicity, or any causal or autoimmune mechanism.

### A conserved architecture of neutrophil cell states (mouse-derived reference)
We confirmed the deposited NeuMap datasets (GSE266680, with GSE141017 and GSE176092; HRA013413) and
resolved 3039 marker genes organizing neutrophil cell states into seven programs (maturation,
interferon-response, immunosuppressive, antigen-presentation and others; Figure 6). The hub markers
are mouse symbols, consistent with a primarily mouse-derived atlas reported to be conserved in humans;
we use it as a reference for how cell states are organized, not as a human layer co-measured with Li or
Chapman. The large quantitative count matrix was not downloaded (a future option). This is state
organization, not executed function. Figure 6 positions NeuMap as a reference architecture for
neutrophil state organization: because it is primarily mouse-derived with reported human conservation,
it serves as an interpretive scaffold rather than a co-measured human layer or proof of cross-layer
coupling.

### A rapid signaling layer, as published evidence
The human phosphosignaling layer is documented at the published-article level (stimulus-specific
signaling under high glucose, homocysteine, LPS). A reusable phosphosite table was not openly available
— it sits inside large vendor binaries (a ~7.7 GB Proteome Discoverer result, a ~6.1 GB .msf), and the
open supplement held no machine-readable tables — so we keep this layer as published evidence of a
rapid signaling state.

### An integrated multi-layer, multi-temporal model
Placed together (Figure 7), these independent datasets suggest that neutrophil-associated inflammatory
biology can be read across complementary layers, each reporting a different biological time and locus
and each from its own cell/species/disease context. The links between layers are biological hypotheses
for paired studies, not measured relationships, and the layers do not share one co-measured severity
scale.

### A minimal observation model shows why NET burden does not identify latent flows
The central limit is most clearly seen in a two-layer observation model (Figure 2). The first layer is
the **process**: extracellular NET burden *B* accumulates from a formation flow *F* and is removed at a
first-order rate *k*, where *k* aggregates enzymatic degradation and systemic clearance (so *k* is the
removal flow decomposed, not an independent axis) and "persistence" is simply the inverse of removal
(longer persistence ⇔ smaller *k*):

> dB/dt = F − kB ,  with steady state  B* = F/k .

The second layer is the **observation**: we never measure *B* directly but a signal *Y = K_R·B + ε*,
where *K_R* is a composition/detectability factor and *ε* is measurement noise — so even a known *B*
need not be reflected faithfully in *Y*. These are two stacked sources of non-identifiability, not one.

From the process layer, **a single cross-sectional scalar measurement of NET burden identifies only the
ratio F/k, not F and k separately** — excess formation and impaired removal are observationally
equivalent. The same ambiguity appears in discrete sampling, where the next burden reflects the
previous burden plus formation minus removal/degradation plus measurement noise.

Two features make this robust rather than a truism. First, the model is deliberately **favourable**:
one compartment, first-order removal, no saturation, no spatial heterogeneity, no detectability bias.
Adding latent processes — DNase saturation, anti-NET shielding, spatial heterogeneity, detection bias —
**without adding measurements cannot resolve the ambiguity; it can only preserve or worsen it**. (A
non-linearity such as saturable removal could in principle restore some structural identifiability
through the transient, but only by adding a distinguishable time-resolved signature that is itself an
extra measurement — reinforcing the point that the remedy is to measure more, not to model more.)
Second, the steady state *B* = F/k* is itself the **most favourable case**: there one at least knows
the system is at equilibrium, and even then a single measurement recovers only F/k. In the realistic
non-stationary setting — NET burden in severe COVID-19 or sepsis is a transient, not an equilibrium —
identifiability is strictly worse, because a single measurement does not even locate where on the
trajectory it was taken.

### NET removal is real and variable, not a mathematical abstraction
The removal flow is not a formal placeholder. Impaired serum NET degradation has been reported in a
subset of systemic lupus erythematosus, attributed to DNase1 inhibitors or anti-NET antibodies that block
DNase1 access, and associated with lupus nephritis (Hakkim et al. 2010, PNAS 107:9813-9818;
DOI: 10.1073/pnas.0909927107; PMID 20439745). In a prospective cohort of 69 SLE patients sampled
approximately every 60 days for a median of 784 days (up to five years), 41% showed reduced
NET-degradation capacity at least once during follow-up (a median of ~20% of their timepoints), and
NET degradation co-varied with disease activity (Leffler et al. 2013, Arthritis Res Ther 15:R84;
DOI: 10.1186/ar4264; PMID 23945056). Because removal varies between patients **and within the same
patient over time**, reading a single NET-burden measurement as formation alone is empirically
indefensible. We do not claim that impaired clearance causes disease, only that the removal flow is
measurable, variable and disease-associated.

### Measurement breakers: the minimal co-measurements that resolve each ambiguity
Because the limit is one of measurement, the constructive output is a set of **measurement breakers** —
for each common ambiguity, the minimal co-measurement that separates the latent mechanisms (Table 2).
For high NET burden, a paired NET-formation assay and a serum NET-degradation/clearance assay on the
same, time-aligned sample separate overproduction from impaired removal; for persistent burden, a
time-course or decay design estimates a removal time-constant rather than a snapshot; for high
NET-associated post-translational modification, a modification-rate or enzyme-activity assay together
with total released material separates a modification process from accumulated modified material; for a
transcriptomic endotype, a same-sample (paired) functional NET-formation readout separates execution
from priming; and for a metabolic state shift, a paired metabolic-plus-functional readout under
perturbation separates a biochemical state from executed output. The breakers are **design requirements, not
demonstrated mechanisms**: each says what to measure to make a mechanism identifiable, not which
mechanism is active.

## Discussion

**Stocks are not flows.** The central point is a measurement one: an accumulated stock-like observable
such as NET burden does not identify the latent flows — formation, degradation, clearance, persistence
— that generate it, and the routine reading of such stocks as mechanistic flows is therefore not
warranted. The mathematics is elementary; the contribution is recognising that this error is common in
inflammatory immunology and turning it into a constructive prescription — the measurement breakers
(Table 2) — for what to co-measure to make each latent mechanism identifiable.

**Dynamic, spatial or agent-based models require these breakers first.** We deliberately do not fit a
dynamic, spatial or agent-based model here: with the available unpaired, cross-sectional public data
such models would be underdetermined rather than mechanistically explanatory. They become identifiable
only once the measurement breakers supply the missing flow measurements; that is a future programme,
not a present claim.

**A supplementary design check.** As a supplementary design check, we used an in silico measurement-design
map under known latent truth to ask which additional measurements reduce which ambiguities collapsed by
burden-only readouts (Supplementary Note; Supplementary Figure). This analysis evaluates measurement-design
identifiability under known latent truth; it does not estimate biological parameter values or patient
mechanisms in the public datasets. It supports the central point that different breakers resolve different
ambiguities while leaving distinct blind spots — burden-only resolves none; a formation assay does not
resolve detectability; and viability and functional execution remain unresolved unless measured directly.

**The neutrophil is not a single-state effector cell.** Around this measurement core, the public layers
describe the neutrophil as a system observed across several molecular layers, each changing on its own
timescale and named by its own cell, species and disease rather than assumed to share one. Each layer
reports something the others do not. The human neutrophil metabolome is the central empirical anchor:
severity is expressed as a shift of the whole metabolic state, with contributors in
glycolysis/PPP/central-carbon metabolism — a state-level signature of severity, not altered flux.

**Each layer adds a distinct kind of information.** The human NET/release layer adds the composition of
what the cell deposits outside itself, with its modifications (including a multiply-represented histone
H3) — relevant because the neutrophil is an effector cell whose biology is partly in what it releases.
The mouse-derived, human-conserved atlas adds how neutrophil cell states are organized, used as a
reference architecture rather than a sample-matched human measurement. The human phosphosignaling layer
adds the fastest, least machine-readable evidence, kept at the published-article level. None of these
substitutes for another, and each is bounded by what its data type can say (composition not rate,
modification presence not enzyme activity, state architecture not executed function, signaling not
downstream effect; see Limitations).

**Two neutrophil architectures converge at the module level (read as context, not coupling).** Read side
by side — and only at the level of modules, not measurements — the two strongest layers describe
hypothesis-compatible activated-neutrophil axes. The severity-associated metabolic state concentrates in a glycolysis/PPP/
central-carbon, redox-linked module (M05), while the released-material layer is structured around oxidative
(MPO), chromatin/histone and granule-protease modules that are themselves post-translationally edited. A
carbon-redox metabolic axis and an oxidative–chromatin–granule effector material are thus two independently
observed architectures of the activated neutrophil that align conceptually at the module level.
This module-level alignment is a hypothesis-generating context alignment, not a measured relationship: because the datasets are not co-measured, it does not imply cross-layer coupling, metabolic flux, NADPH turnover, ROS production, oxidative burst, NETosis, clearance, enzyme activity, pathogenicity, executed function or any causal link, and a paired same-sample design would be required to test it.

**Reading the layers together without pretending they were co-measured.** Because the datasets are
independent, unpaired, and drawn from different cells, species and diseases, combining them into one
measurement would not be warranted, and they do not constitute a shared severity scale. Reading them as
complementary keeps each statement inside the support of its own data and offers cross-layer links as
biological hypotheses. The strength of the approach is precisely this — not pretending all data came
from the same cells, species or disease, but preserving what each source can validly say while still
allowing a biological synthesis. It avoids two common errors: reducing the neutrophil to a single
biomarker, and applying sample-level multi-omic factor integration
(e.g. Argelaguet et al. 2018; DOI: 10.15252/msb.20178124) to datasets that were not co-measured.

## Limitations

In one sentence: because the datasets are independent, we cannot say that the same cell, patient,
species or disease carried the metabolic, released-material, cell-state and signaling features we
describe — we describe each layer on its own and read them together as complementary biology, not as
one combined measurement. Specifically: the datasets are independent and not co-measured, and do not
share a single severity scale (quantitative severity is anchored only in the human neutrophil
metabolomic COVID-19 series, while the released-material layer is human RA/SLE); the metabolic layer is
human neutrophil metabolomics (confirmed from the source) but remains a state, not a flux; the released
proteome and modifications are composition, not a NETosis rate, clearance, or enzyme activity, and
modification co-occurrence on the same peptide/residue is not established; the cell-state layer is a
primarily mouse-derived reference architecture with reported human conservation, used as
transcriptional/regulatory potential and not as a human, sample-matched layer or as executed function;
the phosphosignaling layer is human published evidence, not a reusable phosphosite table; batch and
covariate structure across the independent datasets is unmodeled; and the work is descriptive, with no
validated biomarker and no clinical prediction.

---

## Next validation studies

The cross-layer connections raised here are hypotheses, not results, and require paired or direct
experiments: paired neutrophil multi-omics in the same donors (and same species); same-donor
metabolomics + phosphoproteomics + NET assays; stable-isotope flux for glycolysis/PPP/lactate; direct
NET formation/clearance assays; PAD4/citrullination activity assays; longitudinal severity sampling;
and clinical-baseline incremental-value testing before any predictive claim. A targeted LDHA/lactate/
lactylation follow-up is mentioned only as a conditional future hypothesis, contingent on direct
evidence, and is not part of the present results. The single most decisive future experiment is paired,
same-donor neutrophil multi-omics, which would convert the cross-layer hypotheses raised here into
testable, co-measured relationships.
